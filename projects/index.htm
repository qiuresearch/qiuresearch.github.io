Research Interests

Our current research aims to understand the physical forces animating macromolecular structures and driving biological functions. It has been intellectually exciting and challenging to conduct research at the interface of physics and biology. The daunting complexity of life represents a miraculous success of intricate interplays between multitudes of physical and chemical interactions, such as steric, electrostatic, hydrophobic, and entropic forces. Built upon breathtaking advances in molecular biology, tools of unprecedented sophistication, and knowledge across disciplines, biological physics thrives on quantitative measurements and theoretical understandings of the multitudinous biological processes integral to human health. However, we shall bear in mind that, we are facing an enormously complex system spanning multiscales of structure, energy, and time; collective and persistent efforts from ALL of us are prerequisite to demystifying the living world. Our overarching interest is to apply experimental techniques of physics to study the structure, function, and dynamics of biological molecules and supramolecular assemblies. Our previous research has used quantitative biological scattering techniques and physical theories to good effect: we have first quantified the electrostatic forces between free DNA strands, first demonstrated non-specific divalent cation induced inter-DNA attraction, analyzed the energetics of the first-order DNA condensation, examined the structure and constituent of the spore coat giving rise to its extreme resistance, first discovered divalent induced condensation of triple-strand DNA, and quantified the pressurized DNA storage in bacteriophage lambda.
