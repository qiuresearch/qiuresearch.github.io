Research Projects

      My research interest is to apply experimental techniques of physics to study the structure, function, and dynamics of biological molecules. At first glance, the marvelous machinery of life is simply made up of finite number of molecules and presumably follows the fundamental laws of physics and chemistry that are already known. However, through millions of years of evolution, life has gained daunting complexity and molecules have developed towards perfection beyond the supposed comprehension. This certainly should not stop us from trying. Being a bottom-up approach, probing the underlying physical mechanisms at the molecular level may provide the foundations to unveil the beauty and mystique of the living world.

      DNA, RNA, and protein comprise the central dogma of biology. They can nevertheless all be uniquely described by one-dimensional (1D) chains of either nucleic acids or amino acids. However, their ``native'' configurations are not linear. For example, DNA is tightly folded together in the cell nucleus to achieve ~10,000-fold compaction; RNA or protein must first fold into its specific 3D conformation before taking its structural or catalytic role. Particularly, mis-folding of proteins can be detrimental to life, such as the Alzheimer's disease and the infamous Prion diseases. Thus, understanding the physical forces determining the ``native'' 3D molecular structures is of fundamental scientific importance and contributes to humanity well-being. The governing forces are most likely different for different classes of molecules. DNA is a cylindrical rod with large linear charge density (-1e/1.7A) emphasizing the key role of electrostatics. RNA is also highly charged, but can additionally form intra-chain base pairs and tertiary contacts between domains. Protein is the most complex and also most versatile in functionality. Its amino acid residues can be either negatively or positively charged, and can also own hydrophobic groups. Another important type of molecule is the amphipathic lipid assembling the membrane of a cell and its organelles. Furthermore, living systems are far from equilibrium, maintaining continuous flows of matter, energy, and information. The machinery of life represents a miraculous success of the intricate interplay between multidimensional physical mechanisms such as electrostatic, chemical, mechanical, and entropic forces. Collective scientific approaches and efforts would be required for any attempt of principal studies.

      Working at the interface between biology and physics is both intellectually challenging and exciting. In addition to the knowledge of X-ray and neutron scattering from my graduate and postdoctoral studies, I seek to learn complementary experimental techniques and also develop new methodologies to address biological questions.
