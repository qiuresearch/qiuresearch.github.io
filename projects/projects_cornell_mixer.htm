Microfluidic Mixer

Picture taken from http://people.ccmr.cornell.edu/~lois/flowcell.html
      My work is primarily computational fluid dynamics simulations of microfuidic mixers using commerical software FEMLAB. The prototype of the mixer design is the three inlet and one outlet geometry originally proposed by Knight et al. (Phys. Rev. Lett., 80, 3863, 1998). To eliminate the so called "premixing", two sheath flow channels are added and improve the mixing performance considerably. The goal of numerical simuations is to optimize the performance of rapid mixers by searching for optimal geometrical parameters. Both 2D and 3D simulations have been performed. Please read the following reference for more detail.
  1. H.-Y. Park, X. Qiu, E. Rhoades, J. Korlach, L. W. Kwok, W. R. Zipfel, W. W. Webb, and L. Pollack,
    Achieving uniform mixing in a microfluidic device: hydrodynamic focusing prior to mixing
    Analytical Chemistry, 78(13), 4465-4473 (2006). [html] [pdf]

      Watching the actions of biomolecules in real time demands close synchronization between molecules. The macromolecule conformation changes of our interest are often triggered by addition of ions. To study the fastest events, we then need to introduce the ions to the macromolecule quicky enough, usually in the order of microseconds. The time scale of mixing of two solutions is eventually limited by the final slow diffusion process. Therefore, the logical way to achieve rapid mixing is to reduce the length scale of diffusion, which happens to be of submicron length for microsecond mixing. Such small length scale can be reached with tubulence, which stretches and folds flowing fluids at high Reynolds numbers. This method becomes unfavorable under certain conditions due to large volume consumption and somewhat limited mixing time, ~1 millisecond.

      Alternatively, hydrodynamic focusing can generate submicron jets without incurring tubulence. Two types of geometries have been used. One is via coaxial cylindrical capillaries with inner flow is compressed by the outer fluid. The other is a quasi-2D design in which the solution is squeezed from both sides to submicron size along one direction. As required, the flow is laminar. This design has advantages such as 1) rapid mixing in the order of 10 microseconds, 2) small sample consumption, 3) stable and predictable flow profiles, 4) open geometry compatible with many probing techniques .

      Ideally, mixing starts when the jet is fully developed. However, in reality, the two type of solutions first meet in the focusing region, where the mixing begins. This "premixing" problem haunts the mixer performance because the jet in the focusing region is still rather thick. To overcome this barrier, two diagonal sheath flows are contrived to separate the focusing and mixing. Numerical simulations show that the mixer performance closely approaches the diffusion limit given a specific jet width.
