Biophysics of Nucleic Acids

      This work aims to make some quantitative contributions to the understanding of DNA condensation and RNA folding. The structure and dynamics of nucleic acids is largely dominated by electrostatic interactions due to its polyelectrolyte nature. Of special importance is the role of oppositely charged counterions capable of counteracting the like charge repulsion, e.g., the electrostatic collapse as the early event in RNA folding induced by addition of ions. Intriguingly, even dilute tri- or higher valenced counterions can invert the like charge repulsion into attraction, e.g., the condensation and precipitation of DNA by Cobalt Hexamine or polyamines. Observing the lacking of quantitative measurements, we have chosen a model system (rigid rod-like DNA of 25 base pair long), and quantified the inter-DNA forces in solution.
      Our method is based on the relationship between inter-DNA interacting forces and the DNA-DNA spatial correlations: inter-DNA repulsion promotes a self-avoiding lattice; while inter-DNA attraction leads to molecular "clustering". Figure 1 qualitatively demonstrates the correspondence.
Figure 1: Demonstration of the relationship between inter-DNA forces and their spatial
correlations in term of the structure factor S(Q).

      The DNA-DNA structural correlation S(Q) was measured with small angle X-ray scattering (SAXS) technique; the inter-DNA potential was then obtained using accepted theoretical models of polyelectrolytes. By varying the counterion valence and concentration, we tune the inter-DNA interaction from repulsive to attractive. We find that repulsive interactions dominate up to moderate monovalent ion concentrations, while weak attractions are observed at surprisingly low divalent ion concentrations. Study on DNA-polyamine systems reveals the coexistence and competition of a repulsive fluid phase and an attractive solid phase. Our quantitative results should provide valuable experimental guides to current extensive theoretical studies of nucleic acid structure and dynamics.
Figure 2: Experimental SAXS profiles showing the tuning of inter-DNA forces with DNA concentrations
around 0.6mM. (a) and (c) show the raw I(Q)s with offset for clarity. I(Q)=S(Q)*P(Q), where P(Q) is
the form factor. Dark lines are the fits. (b) and (d) show the S(Q)s corresponding to (a) and (c) respectively.
Weakening of the inter-DNA repulsion is indicated upon increasing salt concentrations. Further more,
the low Q upturns in S(Q) at [Mg2+] > 16mM evidence attraction.

      We are currently studying self-assembled liquid crystalline DNA with x-ray diffraction to measure the forces between DNA strands at the last 1 nanometer DNA-DNA separation.
